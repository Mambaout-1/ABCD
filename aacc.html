What is Anorexia Nervosa? Anorexia nervosa is characterized by extreme food restriction, intense fear of gaining weight, and distorted body image. * Behavioral symptoms: Severely limiting food intake (eating only low-calorie foods), repeatedly counting calories, cutting food into very small pieces; using "purging behaviors" such as excessive exercise, self-induced vomiting, or laxative abuse to compensate for calories; avoiding social meals and developing strange eating rituals (e.g., excessive chewing). * Physical symptoms: Body weight significantly below normal standards, feeling cold, hair loss, dry and yellowish skin, fine body hair (lanugo may appear on the body), loss of menstruation in females; in severe cases, cardiac arrhythmias or even sudden death may occur. * Psychological symptoms: Intense fear of weight gain, self-worth entirely dependent on body weight and shape, denial of the illness. * Treatment focus: Weight restoration and medical stabilization (correcting low weight, cardiac arrhythmias, electrolyte imbalances). Family-based treatment (Maudsley method) is preferred for adolescents; cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT-E) is preferred for adults. Nutritional rehabilitation requires phased weight restoration and closely supervised meals. No specific medication exists; low-dose olanzapine may assist with weight gain and reduce anxiety. What is Bulimia Nervosa? Bulimia nervosa is characterized by recurrent binge eating followed by compensatory behaviors, with body weight typically within normal range or slightly above. * Behavioral symptoms: Recurrent binge eating episodes (e.g., consuming an abnormally large amount of food within 2 hours with a sense of loss of control); immediate compensatory behaviors after eating, such as self-induced vomiting, laxative use, or excessive exercise. Signs of self-induced vomiting include calluses on the knuckles (Russell's sign), swollen parotid glands in the cheeks, and dental erosion. * Psychological symptoms: Intense shame about binge eating, self-evaluation unduly influenced by body weight, anxiety and depression. * Physical symptoms: Electrolyte imbalances (hypokalemia, cardiac arrhythmias), gastrointestinal problems (gastric rupture, constipation), throat injuries. * Treatment focus: Breaking the binge-purge cycle, correcting electrolyte imbalances (especially hypokalemia). CBT-ED (adapted cognitive behavioral therapy) is the first-line treatment. Nutritional rehabilitation focuses on establishing regular eating patterns (three meals plus snacks) and reducing extreme swings between dieting and bingeing. Fluoxetine (Prozac) can effectively reduce binge-purge behaviors. What is Binge-Eating Disorder? Binge-eating disorder is similar to bulimia nervosa but without compensatory behaviors, therefore often accompanied by overweight or obesity. * Behavioral symptoms: Recurrent binge eating episodes, but without self-induced vomiting or laxative use; eating rapidly, eating until uncomfortably full, eating alone due to shame. * Psychological symptoms: Sense of loss of control during binge episodes, intense guilt and distress afterward, but no compensatory behaviors. * Physical symptoms: Often accompanied by overweight or obesity and related metabolic problems (e.g., hypertension, type 2 diabetes). * Treatment focus: Reducing the frequency of binge episodes. CBT-ED (focusing on controlling binge impulses and addressing emotional triggers) is the first-line treatment. Nutritional rehabilitation includes regular eating patterns and weight management (if needed), learning to distinguish between physiological hunger and emotional eating.
