如何治疗 



    

   

    I. Treatment for Anorexia Nervosa
1. Medical Monitoring & Weight Restoration (Highest Priority)
* Indications for hospitalization:
    * Body weight <75% of expected body weight
    * Heart rate <40 bpm
    * Body temperature <35.5°C (95.9°F)
    * Serum potassium <3.0 mmol/L
    * Abnormal ECG
    * Severe hypotension
* Weight restoration goal:
    * Inpatient: 0.5-1 kg (1-2 lbs) per week
    * Outpatient: 0.2-0.5 kg (0.5-1 lb) per week
* Prevention of refeeding syndrome:
    * Start with 800-1200 kcal/day, increase by 200-300 kcal every 1-2 days
    * Supplement with vitamin B1 (thiamine), phosphorus, potassium, magnesium
* Vital sign monitoring:
    * Measure morning heart rate, blood pressure, and body temperature daily
    * Monitor for 1-2 hours after meals to prevent excessive exercise or purging
2. Psychotherapy (Core Treatment)
* First-line for adolescents: Family-Based Treatment (Maudsley Method) – three phases
    * Phase 1 (2-3 months): Parents take full control of patient's eating and weight restoration
    * Phase 2 (2-3 months): Gradually return eating control to the patient
    * Phase 3 (2-3 months): Address adolescent developmental issues (independence, relationships)
* First-line for adults: CBT-E (Enhanced Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) – 40 sessions, twice weekly, approximately 20 weeks
* Other effective therapies:
    * MANTRA (Motivation and Attitude-based treatment for adults)
    * SSCM (Specialist Supportive Clinical Management)
3. Nutritional Rehabilitation
* Meal supervision: Someone stays with the patient during every meal; remain in observation area for 1 hour after eating
* Post-meal management:
    * No immediate access to bathroom (to prevent vomiting)
    * No immediate exercise (to prevent excessive exercise compensation)
* Managing refusal to eat:
    * Allow 30 minutes to finish the meal
    * If incomplete, provide equal-calorie nutritional supplement (e.g., Ensure)
    * If repeatedly refused, consider nasogastric tube feeding (last resort)
4. Medication
* No FDA-approved specific medication
* Auxiliary medication: Low-dose olanzapine (2.5-10 mg/day) may help with weight gain and reduce obsession with body shape
* Caution with SSRIs (e.g., fluoxetine): Not effective for underweight anorexia patients; weight restoration is needed first before assessing for co-occurring depression/anxiety

II. Treatment for Bulimia Nervosa
1. Medical Monitoring
* Emergency management:
    * Hypokalemia (low potassium): Oral or IV potassium replacement
    * Severe hypophosphatemia (low phosphorus): Phosphorus replacement
    * Metabolic alkalosis: IV fluids + potassium replacement
* Dental care:
    * Do NOT brush teeth immediately after vomiting (erodes enamel)
    * Instead, rinse mouth with baking soda solution to neutralize stomach acid
    * Refer to dentist
* Gastrointestinal: Long-term purgers require evaluation for esophagitis and Mallory-Weiss tears (esophageal-gastric junction tears)
2. Psychotherapy (Core & First-Line)
* First-line treatment: CBT-ED (CBT adapted for bulimia) – approximately 20 sessions
    * Phase 1 (weeks 1-6): Establish regular eating patterns (3 meals + 3 snacks), break the binge-purge cycle
    * Phase 2 (weeks 7-12): Identify and change thought patterns that trigger binges (e.g., "I ate one cookie, I might as well eat the whole box")
    * Phase 3 (weeks 13-20): Relapse prevention, develop long-term coping strategies
* Group CBT: Works well for bulimia, reduces cost and provides peer support
3. Nutritional Rehabilitation
* Establish regular eating: Three main meals + three snacks at fixed times each day (never skip meals)
* Stop dieting: Eliminate food rules ("forbidden foods" trigger binges) – gradually reintroduce fear foods
* Keep a food-mood log: Record: time, what you ate, binge/purge or not, thoughts/emotions before and after
* Delay purging after a binge:
    * Start with delaying by 5 minutes → gradually extend to 30 minutes → 1 hour
    * Most patients find the urge to purge decreases after 30-45 minutes
4. Medication
* First-line medication: Fluoxetine (Prozac) – the only FDA-approved medication for bulimia
    * Dosage: 60 mg/day (higher than the 20 mg used for depression)
    * Effect: Can reduce binge-purge frequency by 50-70%
    * Take 4-6 weeks to show effect
* Combination therapy: Medication + CBT works better than either alone
* Other options: Topiramate (second-line, helps reduce binges but has cognitive side effects)

III. Treatment for Binge-Eating Disorder
1. Medical Monitoring
* Weight-related complications: Screen for hypertension, type 2 diabetes, hyperlipidemia, obstructive sleep apnea
* Baseline assessment: Fasting blood glucose, lipid panel, blood pressure, BMI
* No urgent medical stabilization unless complications are severe
2. Psychotherapy (First-Line)
* CBT-ED (for BED) – 20 sessions (most effective)
    * Focus on: Controlling binge impulses, addressing emotional triggers, eliminating dieting mentality
    * Key skill: Distinguishing physiological hunger vs. emotional eating
* Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) – 16-20 sessions
    * Works as well as CBT in the long term
    * Focuses on improving interpersonal relationships that trigger emotional eating
* Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT) – Good for emotional dysregulation
    * Teaches distress tolerance and emotion regulation skills to replace binge eating
3. Nutritional Rehabilitation
* Regular eating pattern: 3 meals + 2-3 snacks at consistent times daily
* Eliminate dieting: "Every time you diet, you set yourself up for the next binge"
* Teach hunger-satiety awareness: Use a hunger scale (1 = starving, 10 = painfully full); aim to eat at 3-4 and stop at 6-7
* Address emotional triggers: Identify emotions that precede binges (boredom, loneliness, anger, anxiety); develop alternative behaviors (call a friend, go for a walk, take a shower)
4. Medication
* FDA-approved: Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) – for moderate to severe BED
    * Dosage: 30-70 mg/day
    * Effect: Reduces binge day frequency
    * Caution: Monitor for increased heart rate, blood pressure, insomnia
* Second-line options: Topiramate (reduces binges and promotes weight loss, but has cognitive side effects like word-finding difficulty)
* For co-occurring depression/anxiety: SSRIs (sertraline, fluoxetine, escitalopram) – effective for reducing binges, but not FDA-approved specifically for BED

General Principles for All Three Disorders
* Family involvement: Especially for adolescents living at home; parents should supervise meals and support recovery
* Level of care escalation:
    * Outpatient → Intensive Outpatient Program (IOP) → Partial Hospitalization Program (PHP) → Inpatient → Residential
* When to hospitalize:
    * Anorexia: Rapid weight loss, severe electrolyte abnormalities, hemodynamic instability (unstable blood pressure/heart rate), suicidality
    * Bulimia: Severe hypokalemia, esophageal tears, cardiac arrhythmias, suicidality
    * BED: Usually does not require hospitalization unless severe obesity complications or suicidality
